## Want to be part of this family? Then start acting like it

In a genre of gangster games where honour mixes with violence and loyalty is more valuable than gold, Mafia: The Old Country promises to take us back to the series' roots. Instead of modern metropolises, we have the picturesque yet perilous Sicily of the early 20^(th) century.

It is here, amid olive groves and stone streets, that Enzo Fava's story begins – that of a young man who embarks on a life of crime in search of a better life. This single-player game is played from a third-person perspective. It was developed by Hangar 13 under the auspices of 2K Games, teams well known to fans of previous instalments in the series.

## Plot: Once you're in, you're never really out

Enzo Fava is a young man from a Sicilian village who, like many before him, wants more than to pick olives and hide from the police. When he enters the world of the Torrisi family, he does not yet realise that 'family' encompasses more than shared meals and relatives in brocade.

But let's be honest — you play the antihero of this early 20^(th)-century tale. Each chapter is a step further down the path of crime for Enzo. Along the way, you'll encounter unpredictable allies and ruthless enemies. This classic gangster story is set against the backdrop of the era's detailed realities, drawing you into the treacherous world of the Mediterranean. Once you get a taste of this world, you'll never want to leave.

## Game world: here, it's survival of the fittest – or you'll end up with teeth in your neck!

Throughout the Mafia series, the settings have been as important as the main characters. Lost Heaven, Empire Bay and New Bordeaux were as important to the series' identity as Tommy, Vito or Lincoln.

Mafia: The Old Country continues this trend by offering a new, memorable setting: early 20^(th)-century Sicily. Its unique topography provides a completely different experience to that of the dense American metropolises of previous instalments. The raw beauty of the Sicilian countryside, with its rural landscapes, cliffs and small towns, contrasts with the dirty alleys, but betrayal, violence and Mafia deals are still rife.

As with every instalment of the series, authenticity was paramount in creating a believable world. The developers at Hangar 13 conducted exhaustive research, analysing rare photos and descriptions from the era and consulting books and films about the Sicilian mafia. Some of the team, accompanied by photographers, also travelled to Italy to gather additional material. They visited various locations, ranging from villages to cities, which were later recreated in the game.

## Game mechanics: Old methods? They never really leave you

Unlike the third instalment, The Old Country does not offer an open world. It is a fully linear story, closer to Mafia I and Mafia II, in which each chapter sees you climb another rung of the mafia ladder – from errand boy to influential figure. But does anyone ever reach the top in stories like this?

You have a selection of authentic pistols, rifles and shotguns (including the lupara!) at your disposal, as well as a knife. Hangar 13 studio has recreated the Sicilian fighting style especially for this game. You can either stab your enemies by surprise or take care of business quickly and loudly. Would you prefer to operate from the shadows? Go ahead. Or prefer a frontal attack? No one will mind – as long as you emerge unscathed.

## Gameplay: On a sunny day, you can momentarily forget what you're really doing

The varied missions take place in settings ranging from Sicilian villages and family villas to the swampy hideouts of smugglers, creating an atmosphere reminiscent of The Godfather with a Palermo twist. Due to the location and time period, foot and horse patrols are commonplace, though some characters use vintage vehicles.

You will visit a variety of locations, ranging from crypts and ruins to vineyards and lavish opera houses. Sometimes, you will have to flee the scene of the crime on horseback; at other times, you will have to escape in a vintage car along narrow gravel roads.

The graphics are a nod to classic gangster cinema. The Old Country resembles an old photograph brought to life. Cigar smoke, hot light bulbs and ominous silence are commonplace here. And the music? An orchestra, a mandolin and a piano – every scene sounds like the final toast before the verdict is delivered.
